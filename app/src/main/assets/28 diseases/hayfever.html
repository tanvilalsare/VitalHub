Allergic Rhinitis (Hay Fever)

What is Hay Fever?

Hay fever, also called allergic rhinitis, causes cold-like symptoms. These may include a runny nose, itchy eyes, congestion, sneezing, and sinus pressure. But unlike a cold, hay fever isn't caused by a virus. It is caused by an allergic response to a harmless outdoor or indoor substance the body identifies as harmful (allergen).

Symptoms

Hay fever symptoms can include:

  • Runny nose and nasal stuffiness, called congestion.
  • Watery, itchy, red eyes.
  • Sneezing.
  • Cough.
  • Itchy nose, roof of mouth or throat.
  • Mucus that runs down the back of the throat, called postnasal drip.
  • Swollen, bruised-appearing skin under the eyes, known as allergic shiners.
  • Extreme tiredness and fatigue, often due to poor sleep.

Hay Fever Triggers

Your hay fever symptoms may occur year-round or may start or worsen at a particular time of year. These are known as seasonal allergies. Hay fever triggers include:

  • Tree pollen, which is common in early spring.
  • Grass pollen, which is common in late spring and summer.
  • Ragweed pollen, which is common in fall.
  • Dust mites and cockroach droppings, which are present year-round.
  • Dander from pets, which can be bothersome year-round but might cause worse symptoms in winter, when houses are closed up.
  • Spores from indoor and outdoor fungi and molds, which can be both seasonal and year-round.

How to Tell Hay Fever from a Common Cold

In hay fever, you don’t get a fever, but in the common cold, there is a low-grade fever involved, in addition to body aches.

Risk Factors

The following can increase a person's risk of developing hay fever:

  • Having other allergies or asthma.
  • Having a condition called atopic dermatitis or eczema, which makes skin irritated and itchy.
  • Having a blood relative, such as a parent or sibling, with allergies or asthma.
  • Living or working in an environment that constantly exposes someone to allergens — such as animal dander or dust mites.
  • Being exposed to smoke and strong odors that irritate the lining of the nose.
  • Having a mother who smoked during the first year of life.

Treatment

Healthcare professionals can help develop a treatment plan for hay fever symptoms, focusing on limiting exposure to triggers and using nonprescription or prescription medicines. Combining allergy medicines can provide the best relief. Children should consult their healthcare professional about treatment, as not all medicines are approved for use in children.

Medications

Some common medications for treating hay fever include:

  • Nonprescription nasal sprays: fluticasone (Flonase Allergy Relief), budesonide (Rhinocort Allergy), triamcinolone (Nasacort Allergy 24HR), mometasone (Nasonex 24HR Allergy).
  • Prescription nasal sprays that combine an antihistamine with a steroid: azelastine and fluticasone (Dymista), mometasone and olopatadine (Ryaltris).
  • Oral antihistamines: loratadine (Claritin, Alavert), cetirizine, fexofenadine (Allegra Allergy).
  • Nonprescription eye drops: olopatadine (Pataday, Patanol), ketotifen (Alaway, Zaditor).
  • Oral decongestants: pseudoephedrine (Sudafed).
  • Nasal decongestant sprays: phenylephrine hydrochloride (Neo-Synephrine), oxymetazoline (Afrin).
  • Cromolyn sodium, Montelukast, Nasal ipratropium, Oral corticosteroids.

Information About Medications

Cromolyn Sodium – Cromolyn sodium works by preventing the release of substances in the body that cause inflammation.

Side Effects

  • Headache, feeling irritable.
  • Nausea, diarrhea, stomach pain.
  • Rash, itching.
  • Muscle pain.

Warning: Inform your doctor if you have kidney disease and/or liver disease.

Montelukast

  • Stomach pain, diarrhea.
  • Fever or other flu symptoms.
  • Ear pain or full feeling, trouble hearing.
  • Headache.
  • Cold symptoms such as runny or stuffy nose, sinus pain, cough, sore throat.
