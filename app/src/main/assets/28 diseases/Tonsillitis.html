Tonsillitis

Overview

Tonsillitis is inflammation of the tonsils, two oval-shaped pads of tissue at the back of the throat, one tonsil on each side. It can be caused by viral or bacterial infections, leading to symptoms such as swollen tonsils, sore throat, difficulty swallowing, and tender lymph nodes in the neck.

Symptoms

Tonsillitis most commonly affects children between preschool ages and the mid-teenage years. Common symptoms include:

  • Red, swollen tonsils
  • White or yellow coating or patches on the tonsils
  • Sore throat
  • Difficult or painful swallowing
  • Fever
  • Enlarged, tender lymph nodes in the neck
  • A scratchy, muffled, or throaty voice
  • Bad breath
  • Stomachache
  • Neck pain or stiff neck
  • Headache

In young children who cannot describe how they feel, signs may include:

  • Drooling due to difficult or painful swallowing
  • Refusal to eat
  • Unusual fussiness

Risk Factors

Risk factors for tonsillitis include:

  • Young age: Tonsillitis most commonly affects children ages 5 to 15, particularly bacterial tonsillitis.
  • Frequent exposure to germs: School-age children are at higher risk due to close contact with peers.

Complications

Inflammation or swelling of the tonsils due to chronic tonsillitis can lead to complications, such as:

  • Obstructive sleep apnea (disrupted breathing during sleep)
  • Tonsillar cellulitis (infection spreading deep into surrounding tissue)
  • Peritonsillar abscess (pus collection behind a tonsil)

If caused by group A streptococcus, untreated or incomplete antibiotic treatment can lead to severe complications such as:

  • Rheumatic fever
  • Complications of scarlet fever
  • Poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis (kidney inflammation)
  • Poststreptococcal reactive arthritis

Prevention

As the germs causing tonsillitis are contagious, practicing good hygiene is essential for prevention. Teach your child to:

  • Wash hands thoroughly and frequently, especially after using the toilet and before eating
  • Avoid sharing food, drinks, water bottles, or utensils
  • Replace their toothbrush after being diagnosed with tonsillitis

To prevent spreading the infection:

  • Keep your child home when sick
  • Ask the doctor when it's safe to return to school
  • Teach your child to cough or sneeze into a tissue or elbow
  • Encourage hand washing after coughing or sneezing

Treatment

At-home care

For viral tonsillitis, at-home care may be sufficient. Consider the following:

  • Encourage rest
  • Ensure adequate fluid intake
  • Offer comforting foods and beverages
  • Prepare a saltwater gargle for soothing relief
  • Use a humidifier to ease throat discomfort
  • Offer lozenges (for older children)
  • Avoid irritants like smoke
  • Administer pain relievers (ibuprofen or acetaminophen) for fever and pain
