Tetanus

What is Tetanus?

Tetanus is a serious disease of the nervous system caused by a toxin-producing bacterium called Clostridium tetani. The disease causes muscle contractions, particularly of your jaw and neck muscles. Tetanus is commonly known as lockjaw.

Symptoms

The average time from infection to appearance of signs and symptoms (incubation period) is 10 days, but it can range from 3 to 21 days. The most common type of tetanus is called generalized tetanus. Signs and symptoms typically begin at the jaw and progressively worsen over two weeks.

  • Painful muscle spasms and stiff, immovable muscles (muscle rigidity) in your jaw.
  • Tension of muscles around your lips, sometimes producing a persistent grin.
  • Painful spasms and rigidity in your neck muscles.
  • Difficulty swallowing.
  • Rigid abdominal muscles.

Localized Tetanus

This uncommon form of tetanus results in muscle spasms near the site of a wound. While it's usually a less severe form of the disease, it can progress to generalized tetanus.

Cephalic Tetanus

This rare form of tetanus results from a head wound. It leads to weakened muscles in the face and spasms of the jaw muscles, and can also progress to generalized tetanus.

Causes

The bacterium that causes tetanus is called Clostridium tetani. It can survive in a dormant state in soil and animal feces until it finds an environment to thrive in, such as a wound.

Risk Factors

  • Not being vaccinated or not keeping up with the 10-year booster shots.
  • Cuts or wounds exposed to soil or manure.
  • A foreign body in a wound, such as a nail or splinter.
  • A history of immune-suppressing medical conditions.
  • Infected skin lesions in people living with diabetes.
  • An infected umbilical cord when a mother isn't fully vaccinated.
  • Shared and unsanitary needles for illegal drug use.

Complications

  • Breathing problems.
  • Blockage of lung artery.
  • Pneumonia.
  • Broken bones.
  • Death.

Prevention

  • Vaccination for children: DTaP (diphtheria, tetanus, and acellular pertussis vaccine).
  • Vaccination for children ages 7-18: Tdap vaccine.
  • Vaccination for adults age 19 and older: Tdap or Td, administered every 10 years.

Treatment

Wound Care

Care for your wound includes cleaning to remove dirt, debris, or foreign objects and clearing the wound of any dead tissue to prevent bacteria growth.

Medications

  • Antitoxin therapy.
  • Sedatives.
  • Vaccination.
  • Antibiotics.

Lifestyle and Home Remedies

Seek medical care for puncture wounds, deep cuts, animal bites, foreign objects, or contaminated wounds. If unsure about tetanus vaccination, seek medical attention. For minor wounds, take these steps:

  • Control bleeding.
  • Clean the wound.
  • Use antibiotics.
  • Cover the wound.
  • Change the dressing.
  • Manage adverse reactions.
