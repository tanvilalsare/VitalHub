Sunburn

Symptoms
  • Inflamed skin, which looks pink or red on white skin and may be harder to see on brown or Black skin.
  • Skin that feels warm or hot to the touch.
  • Pain, tenderness, and itching.
  • Swelling.
  • Small, fluid-filled blisters, which may break.
  • Headache, fever, nausea, and fatigue, if the sunburn is severe.
  • Eyes that feel painful or gritty.
Risk Factors
  • Having white skin and red hair.
  • Having a history of sunburn.
  • Living or vacationing somewhere sunny, warm, or at high altitude.
  • Working outdoors.
  • Swimming or spraying your skin with water or baby oil, as wet skin tends to burn more than dry skin.
  • Mixing outdoor recreation with drinking alcohol.
  • Regularly exposing unprotected skin to UV light from sunlight or artificial sources like tanning beds.
  • Taking photosensitizing medications that make you more likely to burn.
Prevention
  • Avoid sun exposure between 10 a.m. and 4 p.m. to reduce UV rays.
  • Avoid sun tanning and tanning beds.
  • Use water-resistant, broad-spectrum lip balm and sunscreen with an SPF of at least 30.
  • Apply sunscreen generously 30 minutes before going outdoors.
  • Use physical blockers like zinc oxide or titanium dioxide, over other products, except insect repellent.
  • Reapply sunscreen every two hours or more often if swimming or perspiring.
  • Remember that the FDA requires sunscreen to retain its original strength for at least three years.
  • Protect babies and toddlers with brimmed hats and lightweight clothing.
  • If sun-protective clothing and shade aren't available, use sunscreens containing zinc oxide or titanium dioxide.
  • Cover up with umbrellas or wide-brimmed hats.
  • Wear sunglasses with UVA and UVB protection.
  • Be aware of sun-sensitizing medications and cosmetics.
Treatment
  • Sunburn treatment doesn't heal your skin but can ease pain, swelling, and discomfort.
  • If care at home doesn't help or your sunburn is very severe, your health care provider might suggest a prescription corticosteroid cream.
  • For severe sunburn, you might need to be admitted to a hospital for further treatment.
