Chickenpox

Symptoms
  • Fever
  • Loss of appetite
  • Headache
  • Tiredness and a general feeling of being unwell
Phases of the Chickenpox Rash
  • Raised bumps (papules), which break out over a few days.
  • Small fluid-filled blisters (vesicles), which form in about one day and then break and leak.
  • Crusts and scabs, which cover the broken blisters and take a few more days to heal.
Complications
  • Infected skin, soft tissues, bones, joints, or bloodstream caused by bacteria
  • Dehydration
  • Pneumonia
  • Swelling of the brain (encephalitis)
  • Toxic shock syndrome
  • Reye's syndrome, caused by giving aspirin to children and teens
People at Higher Risk of Complications
  • Newborns and infants whose mothers never had chickenpox or the vaccine
  • Teens and adults
  • Pregnant women who haven't had chickenpox
  • People who smoke
  • People with cancer, HIV, or other immune-suppressing conditions
Prevention
  • The chickenpox vaccine (varicella vaccine) is the best way to prevent chickenpox.
  • Two doses of the varicella vaccine prevent illness over 90% of the time.
  • Children receive the first dose between 12-15 months and the second between 4-6 years.
  • The vaccine is especially important for those at higher risk of exposure.
  • Pregnant individuals should not get the chickenpox vaccine.
Treatment
In Otherwise Healthy Children
  • Chickenpox often requires no medical treatment. It typically just needs to run its course.
  • Antihistamines may be used to calm itching.
Lifestyle and Home Remedies
  • Avoid scratching to prevent scarring and slow healing. Trim fingernails and wear gloves at night.
  • Use a cool bath with baking soda, aluminum acetate, or uncooked oatmeal. Apply calamine lotion on itchy spots.
  • Follow a soft, bland diet for mouth sores.
  • Use antihistamines like diphenhydramine (Benadryl) for itching.
  • Use acetaminophen (Tylenol) for mild fever.
  • Consult a provider if fever lasts longer than four days and is higher than 102°F (39°C).
  • Avoid giving aspirin to children and teenagers due to the risk of Reye's syndrome.
  • Consult a provider before giving NSAIDs like ibuprofen to chickenpox patients.
