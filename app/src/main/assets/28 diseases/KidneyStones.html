Kidney Stones

Kidney stones (also known as renal calculi, nephrolithiasis, or urolithiasis) are hard deposits made of minerals and salts that form inside your kidneys.

Symptoms
  • Severe, sharp pain in the side and back, below the ribs
  • Pain that radiates to the lower abdomen and groin
  • Pain that comes in waves and fluctuates in intensity
  • Pain or burning sensation while urinating
  • Pink, red, or brown urine
  • Cloudy or foul-smelling urine
  • A persistent need to urinate, urinating more often than usual or urinating in small amounts
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Fever and chills if an infection is present
Risk Factors
  • Family or personal history of kidney stones
  • Dehydration
  • Certain Diets (e.g., high in oxalates, salt, or animal proteins)
  • Obesity
  • Digestive diseases and surgeries
Prevention
  • Drink plenty of water throughout the day to stay hydrated
  • Eat fewer oxalate-rich foods (spinach, nuts, chocolate, beets)
  • Choose a diet low in salt and animal protein
  • Continue consuming calcium-rich foods but be mindful of calcium supplements
Treatment for Small Kidney Stones with Minimal Symptoms
  • Drinking Water: Drinking 2 to 3 quarts daily can help dissolve small kidney stones.
  • Pain Relievers: Ibuprofen or naproxen sodium can relieve mild pain.
  • Medical Therapy: An alpha blocker (like Flomax or Jalyn) may be recommended to help pass the stone.
Treatment for Large Stones and Those That Cause Symptoms
  • Extracorporeal Shock Wave Lithotripsy (ESWL): Breaks up stones using sound waves.
  • Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy: Surgical removal using telescopes and instruments.
  • Ureteroscopy: Thin tube passed through the urethra and bladder to remove stones.
  • Parathyroid Gland Surgery: For stones caused by an overactive parathyroid gland.
